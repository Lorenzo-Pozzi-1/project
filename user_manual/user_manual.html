Importing Scenarios

To import a scenario, start by clicking the "Import scenario" button. A pop-up will appear, prompting you to pick a file from your computer, remember: the file should either be one auto-generated by [GXCore > Pesticide report > Farm Assurance Application Report > filter by crop-year, country, plant, grower, McCain field name] or one coming from this same app. For now, this app can only read these two formats.

Once selected, the file will be parsed, and you'll see a little summary, plus a pop-up if any product was not recognised in the database. If you see a lot of problems, chances are you selected a region in your preferences, and imported an application report for a different region, so many products could not be matched.

You'll be automatically brought to a second tab where you can fix the matching issues by either skipping them (e.g. adjuvants that do not contribute to EIQ), importing them as-is (you'll see the text, but the row will be excluded from the EIQ calculation), or map it to an existing product searching it by name. The row at the bottom of the pop-up will tell you what's going to happen when you give the "OK".

Once you solved all the product-matching issues, you'll see your applications. If any mistake filtered through when the applications were registered, now you'll see the row light up and be marked as "Incomplete" or "Invalid". If you're not sure why, just hover on the row with the mouse and a little text will pop up telling you what's wrong.

Once all the applications are rectified, you can rename the imported scenario by double clicking the tab name, and then clone it to create a new scenario based on it. Using the "Compare scenarios" button, you can bounce back and forth between views to see what you can do to reduce the EIQ score, or improve/adapt the plan to your grower's needs.

üß™ Environmental Impact Quotient (EIQ).

EIQ scores are used to quantify the environmental impact of pesticides application. Higher scores = higher impacts
It condenses in a single value information about toxicity and exposure risk of farm workers, consumers, and ecological components.
An EIQ score is calculated for each active ingredient based on factors like its toxicity, persistence, and potential for leaching. These values are calcualted by Cornell UNiversity
To compare control products applications however, we use the Field Use EIQ Score: this allows to account fo rthings such as multiple active ingredients, different active ingredient concentrations, and different application rates.
Summing up all the scores for the applications of a season, the full crop protection plan can be evaluated, and different plans can be compared.

What EIQ Measures

Component Risk Category
Farm workers Toxicity to and exposure of applicators and field workers
Consumers Toxicity to and exposure of consumers through food residues and water contamination
Environment Toxicity to and exposure of several non-target species, surface and ground waters contamination

How Field Use EIQ scores are Calculated

Field Use EIQ = Active Ingredient EIQ √ó Active Ingredient Concentration √ó Control Product Application Rate

Score Interpretation

Field Use EIQ Environmental Impact Typical Potato Applications
< 20 Lower impact Biologicals, modern targeted chemistries, etc.
20-50 Moderate impact Average fungicides, herbicides, and insecticides, etc.
50-100 Higher impact Old and/or broad spectrum chemistries, high application rates, etc.
> 100 Very high impact Highly toxic products, fumigants, very high application rates, etc.

üöú Soil Tillage Intensity Rating (STIR).

The STIR quantifies the intensity of tillage operations. This affects their impact on erosion potential, soil organic matter preservation, and thus overall health and fertility. Higher STIR scores = higher soil disturbance.

What STIR Measures

Factor Assessment Focus
Tillage type Inversion, lifting, mixing, compression, etc.
Speed The faster an implement runs through the soil, the more it disturbs
Working depth The deeper an implement works, the more soil it disturbs
Surface area disturbed An implement working on a limited area disturbs less soil (e.g. strip tillage)

How STIR is Calculated

STIR = (tillage type x 3.25) x (speed x 0.5) x (depth) x (surface area disturbed)

the STIR is calcualted and summed for each operation executed in a full crop rotation, then divided by the number of years of the ortation to get the average yearly STIR. this is important as soil disturbace has to be kept as minimal as possible throughouth multiple years in order to deliver any benefit.
